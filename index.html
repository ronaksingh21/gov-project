Advaith's argument

AI causes civil rights harms existing law can't fix

Should the federal government regulate AI in hiring and criminal justice?

AI hiring tools cause documented discrimination — courts are catching it after the fact, not preventing it

  • AI tools trained on biased historical data replicate discrimination through "proxy variables" — zip codes, college names — disparate impact without ever using race directly
    Source 1 · ACLU
  • Mobley v. Workday — federal court let discrimination claims proceed against an AI vendor directly. Victims must sue after the fact. No prevention mechanism exists
    Source 3 · Holland & Knight
  • DOJ 2024 report flagged facial recognition, risk assessment tools, and predictive policing as high civil rights risk — government confirmed the problem, but has no enforcement body
    Source 2 · DOJ Final Report
  • Biden's EO 14110 created rights-impacting AI framework → Trump's EO 14148 revoked it entirely. Executive action alone is too unstable — only a commission established by law survives administrations
    Source 3 · Holland & Knight
Source 1 · ACLU Source 2 · DOJ Report Source 3 · Holland & Knight

The "black box" problem violates due process — defendants can't challenge what they can't see

  • Risk assessment tools in criminal justice are proprietary — defendants can't see the algorithm's reasoning, which undermines the due process right to challenge evidence
    Source 2 · DOJ Final Report
  • Predictive policing raises 4th Amendment questions; racially disparate sentencing outputs raise 14th Amendment equal protection claims — these are government action problems requiring a federal response
    Source 2 · DOJ Final Report
  • Illinois and Texas have conflicting state AI civil rights laws — your protection depends on which state you live in. That itself is an equal protection problem
    Source 3 · Holland & Knight
  • EEOC pulled back its own AI hiring guidance — proves existing agencies have already chosen not to self-regulate
    Source 3 · Holland & Knight
Source 2 · DOJ Report Source 3 · Holland & Knight
Ronak's argument

The regulatory gap is proven — no existing agency can fill it

Should the US establish a dedicated federal AI safety agency?

Congress has tried and failed — no law, no agency, no consistent enforcement

  • Congress introduced hundreds of AI bills — fewer than 30 passed, zero created a dedicated agency. Nonpartisan CRS statistic, impossible to dispute
    Source 1 · CRS Report
  • AISI was renamed CAISI by Trump and narrowed to national competitiveness only — safety, fairness, and bias removed from mandate. Any body without statutory authority can be gutted by the next president
    Source 2 · Brookings + Source 3 · NIST
  • No federal law = state patchwork. Texas, Illinois, California, Utah all have different standards — unequal protection based on zip code
    Source 1 · CRS Report
  • The TRAINS Taskforce (DOD, DOE, DHS, NIH) already does coordinated federal AI evaluation for national security — proof of concept that a multi-agency structure works. Needs to expand to civil/commercial AI
    Source 3 · NIST
Source 1 · CRS Report Source 2 · Brookings Source 3 · NIST

The FAA doesn't ban planes — it certifies them. An AI commission should work the same way

  • EU AI Act classifies employment AI as "high-risk" — requires pre-deployment testing. US companies in the EU already comply with EU rules, with zero equivalent protection for Americans at home
    Source 1 · CRS Report
  • UK tried sector-by-sector approach without a new agency — CRS notes it creates inconsistency. The cautionary tale of what happens when you don't act
    Source 1 · CRS Report
  • Brookings: without a technical federal agency, the US cannot set international AI standards — ceding that ground to the EU or China directly undercuts "American leadership"
    Source 2 · Brookings
  • Trump's own Dec. 2025 EO calls AI central to national and economic security — flip it: if AI is that critical, it deserves the same dedicated oversight as aviation (FAA) or nuclear energy (NRC)
    Source 4 · Trump EO
Source 1 · CRS Report Source 2 · Brookings Source 4 · Trump EO
Policy timeline

How we got here

Key events in US AI regulation

Nov 2023
Biden EO 14110
Directed agencies to assess AI civil rights risks. Created the US AI Safety Institute (AISI) inside NIST.
Nov 2024
TRAINS Taskforce
NIST forms interagency AI evaluation group including DOD, DOE, DHS, NIH for national security risks.
Jan 2025
EO 14148 — Biden order revoked
Trump revokes EO 14110. Signs EO 14179 ("Removing Barriers to American Leadership in AI"). EEOC AI guidance pulled back.
Mar 2025
Mobley v. Workday
Federal court allows discrimination claims against an AI hiring vendor to proceed — first major case holding an AI company directly liable.
Jun 2025
AISI → CAISI
AI Safety Institute renamed and refocused on competitiveness only. Safety and bias evaluation removed from mandate.
Jul 2025
Senate votes 99–1
Senate blocks proposed 10-year moratorium on state AI laws. States can keep passing their own rules.
Dec 2025
Trump AI framework EO
National AI policy focused on removing regulatory obstacles. Creates AI Litigation Task Force to challenge state AI laws in court.
Today
No comprehensive federal AI law
Hundreds of bills introduced. Fewer than 30 passed. Zero created a dedicated AI safety agency.
Global comparison

How other countries regulate AI

The US is the only major economy with no comprehensive AI law

🇪🇺
European Union
Strongest regulation

EU AI Act (2024) — risk-tiered. Employment AI = high-risk, requires pre-deployment testing and registration. Unacceptable risk = banned outright.

🇬🇧
United Kingdom
Sector-by-sector

No new agency. Existing regulators apply existing powers to AI in their sector. CRS notes this creates inconsistency — the cautionary tale.

🇺🇸
United States
No comprehensive law

Relies on EOs, voluntary commitments, and existing agencies. CAISI exists but has no rulemaking authority. State patchwork fills the gap unevenly.

🇨🇳
China
State-controlled

Vertical, technology-specific approach. Strong government control over AI content and use. Driven by national security and economic development goals.

The argument from competition

US companies operating in the EU must already comply with EU AI rules — with zero equivalent protection for Americans at home. If the US doesn't set international AI standards, the EU or China will. A federal AI commission is how the US leads, not how it falls behind.
